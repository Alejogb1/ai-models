# Part 1: Moore — Crossing the Chasm

## 1.1 Core Adoption Framework

### [C01] Technology Adoption Lifecycle
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** Moore's full definition of the bell curve (innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards). The percentages for each segment. The key insight that the curve describes how *populations* adopt technology, not individuals.
- **Search hints:** "bell curve," "adoption lifecycle," "technology diffusion"

### [C02] The Chasm
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2, title concept
- **Need from you:** Moore's definition of the chasm as the gap between early adopters and early majority. Why it exists (different psychology of these two groups). What happens when companies fail to cross it.
- **Search hints:** "the chasm," "crack in the bell curve," "the gap between visionaries and pragmatists"

### [C03] Visionaries (Early Adopters)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** Moore's characterization of early adopters: what they buy, why they buy, what they expect. The "revolutionary" mindset. Their low price sensitivity but high expectation of breakthrough.
- **Search hints:** "early adopters," "visionaries," "revolutionaries"

### [C04] Pragmatists (Early Majority)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** Moore's characterization of the early majority: what they buy, why they buy, what they expect. The "evolutionary" mindset. Reference-buying behavior. "Show me someone else who has done this successfully."
- **Search hints:** "early majority," "pragmatists," "reference buyers"

### [C05] Conservatives (Late Majority)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** Moore's characterization of late majority. Their price sensitivity. Their preference for mature, standardized solutions. Their risk aversion.
- **Search hints:** "late majority," "conservatives," "skeptics"

### [C06] Skeptics (Laggards)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** Moore's characterization of laggards. The "just say no" attitude. Why they don't matter for mainstream adoption.
- **Search hints:** "laggards," "skeptics"

## 1.2 Market Development Strategy

### [C07] The Bowling Alley
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 5
- **Need from you:** Moore's metaphor of bowling pins as niche markets. The strategy of knocking over one pin (dominate one niche), which knocks into the next (creates reference for adjacent niches). The sequence of niche domination before mainstream.
- **Search hints:** "bowling alley," "bowling pin strategy," "niche by niche"

### [C08] The Tornado
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 6
- **Need from you:** Moore's definition of the tornado phase: hypergrowth when the mainstream market adopts en masse. What triggers it. What it looks like operationally. The "land grab" mindset.
- **Search hints:** "tornado," "mass market adoption," "hypergrowth"

### [C09] Main Street
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 7
- **Need from you:** Moore's post-tornado phase where growth stabilizes. The focus shifts from customer acquisition to account management, customer retention, and product line extension.
- **Search hints:** "main street," "after the tornado," "mature market"

## 1.3 Whole Product & Positioning

### [C10] Whole Product Concept
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3–4
- **Need from you:** Moore's adaptation of the whole product model: generic product → expected product → augmented product → potential product. The insight that different adoption segments need different levels of "whole product."
- **Search hints:** "whole product," "generic product," "augmented product," "product completeness"

### [C11] Compelling Reason to Buy (Value Proposition)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3–4
- **Need from you:** Moore's definition of the compelling reason to buy for each adoption category. What drives a visionary to buy vs. what drives a pragmatist. The "target customer" + "compelling reason to buy" combination.
- **Search hints:** "compelling reason to buy," "value proposition," "target customer"

### [C12] Positioning as Competition
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 8
- **Need from you:** Moore's positioning framework: (1) claim the competitive category, (2) differentiate your entry, (3) establish your credentials. The four-step positioning process.
- **Search hints:** "positioning," "competitive category," "differentiation," "credentials"

### [C13] The Whole Product in the Chasm
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 4
- **Need from you:** Moore's specific argument about the whole product during the chasm crossing. Why the whole product must be 100% complete for the early majority but not for early adopters. The "whole product gap" analysis.
- **Search hints:** "whole product gap," "whole product during the chasm"

## 1.4 Organizational Implications

### [C14] The D-Day Analogy
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 4
- **Need from you:** Moore's D-Day / beachhead metaphor. The idea of concentrating resources on a single, narrow market segment to establish a foothold before expanding. The four principles: (1) narrow point of attack, (2) single market focus, (3) create a "special force," (4) limited objective.
- **Search hints:** "D-Day," "beachhead," "narrow point of attack"

### [C15] Segment-Based Marketing
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3
- **Need from you:** Moore's argument against broad market segmentation (demographics, psychographics). His alternative: situation-based segmentation that identifies *when* a customer needs a solution, not *who* they are.
- **Search hints:** "situation-based segmentation," "market segmentation," "needs-based segmentation"

### [C16] The Inside-View Bias
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** Moore's warning about technologists/developers misjudging the market because they evaluate from an insider perspective. The gap between what engineers think is compelling and what the market will buy.
- **Search hints:** "inside view," "technologist bias," "marketing myopia"

### [C17] The Two-Phase Go-to-Market
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 4
- **Need from you:** Moore's distinction between the early market go-to-market (sell to visionaries, product-centric) and the mainstream go-to-market (sell to pragmatists, market-centric). The transition between the two.
- **Search hints:** "early market go-to-market," "mainstream go-to-market," "two-phase"

# Part 2: Christensen — The Innovator's Dilemma

## 2.1 Disruption Theory

### [C18] Sustaining Innovation
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** Christensen's definition: innovations that improve existing products along dimensions historically valued by mainstream customers. Typically introduced by incumbents. Can be incremental or radical while still being "sustaining."
- **Search hints:** "sustaining innovation," "sustaining technology," "incremental innovation"

### [C19] Disruptive Innovation (Original Definition)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** Christensen's original definition: innovations that underperform on the dimensions mainstream customers care about, but introduce a different value proposition — typically simpler, cheaper, more accessible. The asymmetry of motivation: disruptors are *not* threatening to incumbents because the market is too small.
- **Search hints:** "disruptive innovation," "disruptive technology," "cheaper, simpler, more accessible"

### [C20] Performance Oversupply
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2, 5
- **Need from you:** Christensen's observation that in most markets, incumbents overshoot customers' needs over time. They add features/performance beyond what the market will pay for. This opens the low end to disruptors who offer "good enough."
- **Search hints:** "performance oversupply," "overshoot," "too much performance"

### [C21] The Disruptive Trajectory
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1–2
- **Need from you:** The diagram/description of how disruptors start at the low end, improve predictably, and eventually meet mainstream needs while the incumbent is trapped in unprofitable high-end segments.
- **Search hints:** "disruptive trajectory," "disruptor's improving performance," "upmarket migration"

### [C22] Asymmetric Motivation
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** The reason incumbents ignore disruptors: the disruptor's initial market is too small to matter to the incumbent's growth targets. The incumbent is *rationally* motivated to ignore the entrant. This is the "innovator's dilemma."
- **Search hints:** "asymmetric motivation," "small markets don't solve big companies' growth needs," "rational to ignore"

### [C23] Value Network
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3
- **Need from you:** Christensen's concept: the context within which a firm competes — its customers, suppliers, cost structure, and business model. A firm's value network determines which innovations are "attractive" and which are "threatening." Disruption often requires moving to a different value network.
- **Search hints:** "value network," "value chain," "context of competition"

## 2.2 Resource Allocation & Organizational Failure

### [C24] Resource Dependence Theory
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 5
- **Need from you:** The idea that organizations allocate resources based on their most demanding customers (who provide the resources). Managers have less discretion than they think — customers dictate strategy through resource flows.
- **Search hints:** "resource dependence," "customers allocate resources," "who pays decides"

### [C25] The Resource Allocation Process
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 5
- **Need from you:** Christensen's description of the RAP: innovation proposals must pass through organizational filters — financial hurdles, strategic fit with current customers, alignment with existing processes and values. This systematically filters out disruptive innovations.
- **Search hints:** "resource allocation process," "RAP," "organizational filters"

### [C26] Organizational Capabilities Framework (RPV)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 6
- **Need from you:** Christensen's RPV framework: Resources (what you have) → Processes (how you do things) → Values (what you prioritize). Disruption requires different RPV configurations. Incumbents' processes and values are their greatest liabilities in the face of disruption.
- **Search hints:** "RPV," "resources, processes, values," "organizational capabilities"

### [C27] The Innovator's Dilemma (Core Statement)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1, Ch. 11
- **Need from you:** The definitive statement of the dilemma: "doing the right thing (listening to best customers, investing in most profitable segments) is exactly what causes incumbents to fail." The paradox of good management.
- **Search hints:** "innovator's dilemma," "good management is the cause," "listening to customers leads to failure"

### [C28] Technology Mudslides
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 10
- **Need from you:** The mudslide analogy: disruptive threats are like mudslides that happen so slowly/far away that the organization can't muster a response. By the time the threat is visible, it's too late.
- **Search hints:** "mudslide," "slow-moving crisis," "boiled frog"

## 2.3 Market & Customer Dynamics

### [C29] Small Markets Don't Solve Large Companies' Growth Needs
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** The canonical argument: a $50M company needs ~$10M in new revenue; a $5B company needs ~$500M. The disruptor's initial market is too small to be interesting. This is the root cause of asymmetric motivation.
- **Search hints:** "small markets," "growth needs," "$800 million market"

### [C30] Failed Disruption (Empirical Examples)
- **Canonical source:** Throughout
- **Need from you:** Christensen's examples of disruption that *failed* or was misapplied. What factors made it fail? Overreach? Wrong market timing? Too much VC funding too fast?
- **Search hints:** "failed disruption," "why disruption fails"

### [C31] The Extinction Principle
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 10
- **Need from you:** The observation that 90%+ of disruptive entrants also fail. The disruptor also faces the dilemma — they need to find a business model that works. No guarantee of success for the disruptor either.
- **Search hints:** "disruptors fail too," "extinction," "most new ventures fail"

## 2.4 Strategy & Response

### [C32] Autonomous Business Unit
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 6
- **Need from you:** Christensen's prescription for incumbents facing disruption: create an autonomous organization with the right RPV configuration — separate processes, separate values, separate resource allocation. The unit must be free from the incumbent's overhead.
- **Search hints:** "autonomous business unit," "autonomous organization," "spin-off"

### [C33] Discover vs. Deliver Strategy
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 5, 9
- **Need from you:** The distinction between a *deliberate* strategy (plan, execute) and an *emergent* strategy (discover through action, then formalize). Disruption requires emergent/discovery approach because the market doesn't exist yet.
- **Search hints:** "deliberate strategy," "emergent strategy," "discovery-driven planning"

### [C34] The Right Size of the Opportunity
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 9
- **Need from you:** Christensen's framework for matching the size of the venture to the size of the opportunity. Too much funding kills disruptive ventures; too little starves them. The "right size" is the smallest that can succeed.
- **Search hints:** "right size the opportunity," "appropriate funding," "smallest that can succeed"

### [C35] The "Jobs to Be Done" Theory
- **Canonical source:** Various (also formalized in *Competing Against Luck*)
- **Need from you:** The concept that customers don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. The functional, social, and emotional dimensions of the "job." This connects disruption theory to user need analysis.
- **Search hints:** "jobs to be done," "JTBD," "hire a product to do a job"

# Part 3: Osterwalder — Business Model Generation

## 3.1 Business Model Canvas (The Nine Building Blocks)

### [C36] Customer Segments (CS)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: the different groups of people or organizations an enterprise serves. Mass market vs. niche market vs. segmented vs. diversified vs. multi-sided platforms.
- **Search hints:** "customer segments," "mass market," "niche market," "segmented"

### [C37] Value Propositions (VP)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: the bundle of products/services that create value for a specific customer segment. The value elements: newness, performance, customization, "getting the job done," design, brand/status, price, cost reduction, risk reduction, accessibility, convenience/usability.
- **Search hints:** "value proposition," "value elements," "creating value for customer segment"

### [C38] Channels (CH)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: how a company communicates with and reaches its customer segments to deliver a value proposition. The five phases: awareness → evaluation → purchase → delivery → after-sales. Direct vs. indirect channels. Partner vs. owned channels.
- **Search hints:** "channels," "distribution channels," "channel phases"

### [C39] Customer Relationships (CR)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: the types of relationships a company establishes with specific customer segments. Categories: personal assistance, dedicated personal assistance, self-service, automated services, communities, co-creation.
- **Search hints:** "customer relationships," "relationship types," "customer acquisition, retention, upsell"

### [C40] Revenue Streams (RS)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: the cash a company generates from each customer segment. Pricing mechanisms: asset sale, usage fee, subscription, lending/renting/leasing, licensing, brokerage fees, advertising. Fixed vs. dynamic pricing.
- **Search hints:** "revenue streams," "pricing mechanisms," "revenue model"

### [C41] Key Resources (KR)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: the most important assets required to make a business model work. Physical, intellectual, human, financial.
- **Search hints:** "key resources," "physical resources," "intellectual resources"

### [C42] Key Activities (KA)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: the most important things a company must do to make its business model work. Production, problem solving, platform/network.
- **Search hints:** "key activities," "production," "problem solving," "platform"

### [C43] Key Partnerships (KP)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: the network of suppliers and partners that make the business model work. Optimization and economy of scale, reduction of risk and uncertainty, acquisition of particular resources and activities.
- **Search hints:** "key partnerships," "supplier network," "strategic alliances"

### [C44] Cost Structure (CS)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 1
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: all costs incurred to operate a business model. Cost-driven vs. value-driven. Fixed costs, variable costs, economies of scale, economies of scope.
- **Search hints:** "cost structure," "cost-driven," "value-driven," "economies of scale"

## 3.2 Business Model Patterns

### [C45] Unbundling Business Model
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** The concept of unbundling three "core businesses" (customer relationship, product innovation, infrastructure) into separate entities. Application to Deepl Search: what gets unbundled from traditional ad platforms?
- **Search hints:** "unbundling," "three core business types," "customer relationship business"

### [C46] The Long Tail
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** Business model pattern focused on selling a large number of niche items (each with low volume) rather than a small number of blockbusters. The key enablers: low inventory costs, strong search/recommendation, democratized production.
- **Search hints:** "long tail," "niche products," "blockbusters vs. niches"

### [C47] Multi-Sided Platforms (MSP)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's definition: platforms that bring together two or more distinct but interdependent groups of customers. The value for each side depends on the presence of the other side. Network effects. The "chicken-and-egg" problem.
- **Search hints:** "multi-sided platform," "MSP," "two-sided market," "network effects"

### [C48] FREE as a Business Model
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** The freemium pattern: offering basic services for free while charging for premium features. Cross-subsidy patterns. The "attention economy" variant (ads pay for free content).
- **Search hints:** "FREE," "freemium," "cross-subsidy," "free + premium"

### [C49] Open Business Models
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 2
- **Need from you:** The pattern of collaborating with external partners to create and capture value. Open innovation (P&G "Connect + Develop"), open source, platform ecosystems.
- **Search hints:** "open business model," "open innovation," "outside-in innovation"

## 3.3 Strategy & Environment

### [C50] SWOT Analysis for Business Models
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 4
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's adaptation of SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) applied to each of the nine building blocks. How to identify weaknesses in a business model by analyzing each block.
- **Search hints:** "SWOT," "business model strengths," "business model weaknesses"

### [C51] Blue Ocean Strategy
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 4
- **Need from you:** The connection between business model innovation and blue ocean strategy (Kim & Mauborgne). Creating new market space (blue ocean) vs. competing in existing markets (red ocean). The value innovation framework.
- **Search hints:** "blue ocean," "red ocean," "value innovation," "new market space"

### [C52] Business Model Environment
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 4
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's four forces: market forces (customer segments, needs, demand), industry forces (competitors, new entrants, substitutes), key trends (tech, regulatory, social, economic), and macroeconomic forces (capital markets, commodities, economic conditions).
- **Search hints:** "business model environment," "market forces," "industry forces," "key trends"

### [C53] Scenario Planning for Business Models
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 4
- **Need from you:** Using multiple future scenarios (e.g., optimistic, pessimistic, transformative) to stress-test a business model. The process of scenario building and business model adaptation.
- **Search hints:** "scenario planning," "future scenarios," "stress test business model"

## 3.4 Process & Design

### [C54] Customer Insights (Empathy Map)
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's adaptation of the empathy map: what the customer sees, hears, thinks/feels, says/does. The pains and gains framework. How to use this for customer profile design.
- **Search hints:** "empathy map," "customer insights," "pains and gains"

### [C55] Ideation Techniques
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's approach to business model ideation: "what if" questions, ignoring constraints initially, using the Canvas as a shared language. Idea generation vs. selection.
- **Search hints:** "ideation," "brainstorming business models," "what if"

### [C56] Prototyping Business Models
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's concept of business model prototyping: napkin sketch → business case → field test. Each prototype is a different level of fidelity. The goal is learning, not validation.
- **Search hints:** "prototyping," "business model prototype," "napkin sketch," "field test"

### [C57] Business Model Storytelling
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 3
- **Need from you:** Using narrative and visual storytelling to communicate a business model. The "business model story" framework: customer profile → value proposition → how it works → financial viability.
- **Search hints:** "storytelling," "business model story," "narrative"

## 3.5 Evaluation & Management

### [C58] Business Model Scorecard
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 5
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's evaluation criteria: P&L viability, external environment fit, internal consistency. The "strengths and weaknesses" per building block.
- **Search hints:** "scorecard," "business model evaluation," "viability criteria"

### [C59] Business Model Portfolio
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 5
- **Need from you:** The idea that companies can (and should) run multiple business models simultaneously. Exploration vs. exploitation models. "Innovation portfolio" applied to business models.
- **Search hints:** "business model portfolio," "multiple business models," "exploration vs. exploitation"

### [C60] Managing Business Models Over Time
- **Canonical source:** Ch. 5
- **Need from you:** Osterwalder's lifecycle perspective: design → implement → manage → evolve. The need to revisit the Canvas periodically. When to pivot vs. when to optimize.
- **Search hints:** "business model lifecycle," "evolve business model," "pivot"

# Part 4: Cross-Cutting Synthesis Concepts

These aren't from any single book but emerge from combining the three frameworks. I derive these once the raw source passages are extracted.

### [X01] Adoption-Viability Matrix
Combining Moore's adoption lifecycle with Christensen's disruption theory: a 2×2 grid (segment × disruption type) that maps which business models work for which stage.

### [X02] Whole Product for the Chasm + Jobs to Be Done
The intersection of Moore's whole product and Christensen's JTBD: "the whole product is what gets the job done." This is the operational test of product completeness.

### [X03] Business Model Canvas + RPV
Mapping Christensen's RPV framework onto the Canvas: Resources = Key Resources + Key Partnerships; Processes = Key Activities; Values = Customer Segments + Customer Relationships + Value Propositions.

### [X04] Viability Threshold
The minimum market size at which a product can sustain itself (from Moore's chasm economics + Christensen's "right size the opportunity"). Applied to Deepl Search: what's the minimum query volume / customer count for viability?

### [X05] Platform Business Model + Multi-Sided Intent
Connecting Osterwalder's MSP pattern to Deepl Search's two-sided market (users + advertisers + LLMs as a third side). The network effect between query volume, LLM quality, and advertiser ROI.

### [X06] The Pruning Ratio as an Economic Law
3.08:1 → only 31% of intent classes are commercially viable. This is Christensen's "asymmetric motivation" applied to advertising: advertisers are rationally motivated to ignore long-tail intent classes.

### [X07] The Octohedral Taxonomy as Infrastructure
The 8→64→512→3,843 structure as a "key resource" (Osterwalder) that enables the whole business model. Like a patent portfolio or a platform — it's an asset that becomes more valuable as more parties use it.
